Qualitative Methods

Goal of Qualitative Inquiry


To gain a deep, contextual understanding of an issue from the perspective of those involved or affected

Qualitative VS Quantitative

Qualitative Quantitative
To describe variation To quantify variation
Prioritizes depth (Often) prioritizes breadth
Answers why/how Answers how much/many
To test hypotheses To develop hypotheses
Highly flexible Often rigid, standardized
Textual Numeric

Qualitative AND Quantitative

Order Description
Qual ︎➡️ Quant Measure development then validation, hypothesis generation then testing (Exploratory Sequential)
Quant ➡️ Qual Collect qualitative data to better understand quantitative results (Explanatory Sequential)
Quant 🔁 Qual Collect and analyze both types of data simultaneously and look for convergence (Convergent)

Methods

Method Best For
Interviews Collecting personal narratives and perspectives, understanding sensitive topics
Focus groups Understanding group norms, competing perspectives
Participant observation Observing naturally occurring behavior in context

Interviews

Know Your Role

  • The interviewer is the student, and the person being interviewed is the expert
  • The goal is for the student to learn as much as the expert is willing to share about a topic
  • The student poses open-ended questions, listens attentively, and probes to gain understanding

Key skills

  • rapport building
  • emphasize other perspective
  • adapt to personality/state
  • active listening

Active Listening

Direct Probes Indirect Probes
Can you say more about that? Verbal expressions: uh huh, ok, i see
I’m not sure I understand. Can you explain? Restatement: “So you are saying that…”
Can you give me an example? Reflection: “And that made you feel…”
Why do you think…“? Non-verbal gestures such as nodding

G2R Free Listing/Sorting

  • We conducted 8 focus groups with 38 randomly selected coaches and participants to elicit perspectives on how coaches contribute to participant success
  • In the free-listing portion, we asked discussion participants to list all of the characteristics of coaches—and facets of coaching—that they think are important, and the co-facilitator documented these ideas on individual index cards.
  • After free listing, the facilitator asked the group to sort the cards in order of importance.

  • The 8 groups free-listed 137 cards, 17.1 cards per group on average.
  • We removed 26 duplicate cards within groups and then collapsed similar terms across groups for a total of 32 unique cover terms.
  • We then scored each cover term based on the rankings across groups and created the overall ranking by group type.

Direct Observation

⚠️ This Not Participant Observation

Today we’ll talk about direct observation which sounds similar to a third qualitative data collection method called participant observation.

Participant observation is a time-consuming enthnographic technique where the goal is to learn the perspectives and behaviors of a target population through observation and interaction.

The goal of direct observation is to quantify or rate the observable behaviors of a target population.

Observational Measures

Often considered a “gold standard” measure of behavior because:

  1. it reduces bias present in methods where someone reports on their own behavior; and
  2. allows for a more detailed understanding of how, when, and why a behavior occurs.

Observational Measures

Can be used for:

  • Descriptive research (to understand an issue)
  • Intervention research (to document the process and outcomes)
    • How was the intervention delivered? (fidelity, competency)
    • What was the outcome of the intervention?

How was the Intervention Delivered?

Fidelity

Key question: Was the intervention delivered like it was designed/intended?1

Example: Did coaches follow the manual?

  1. Can ask for self-report, which is then not observational; having a separate observer is much more valid; a selection of sessions can be observed rather than all.

Competency

Key question: How skillfully did providers deliver the intervention?

Example: Did coaches use effective strategies when doing the steps?

Example measure: Competency rating tool

In a survey, we can multiple choice questions to ask for ratings (e.g., Not at all, A Little, Very).

In an observational measure, judgments are based on watching/listening to delivery (live or recorded). The measure tells you which behaviors to look and listen for.

What was the Outcome of the Intervention?

Direct Observation of Outcomes

Target Example
Individual Behavior child’s classroom behavior (e.g., track how many times they talk without permission)
Interactions parent-child interactions, family interactions

Direct Observation of Outcomes

Methods:

  • Naturalistic (watching/recording in the natural environment, e.g., home or school)
  • Structured, standardized activities as part of an assessment
    • Give participants a standardized prompt while watching or recording
    • Count or describe specific behaviors

Example: Parent-Child Interactions

Setting: Parenting intervention evaluation in Liberia

Setup: A square on the ground using pieces of rope and locally-available materials that children commonly play with (rocks, paper, pencils/crayons, bottles, boxes). Tablets used to record 5-min video interaction.

Prompt: “Let child be free in the play and follow along with what your child wants to do.”

Rated from recording: (1) Level of Activity/Interaction, (2) Parent Praising the child, (3) Positive Child Reactions, (4) Child Verbalizations

Example: Family Interactions

Setting: Family therapy intervention evaluation in Kenya

Structured family activities (video recorded; 10 min each):

  • Hands-on problem-solving task (“house” building)
  • Family discussion prompt about hopes
  • Couples conflict discussion prompt

We rated:

  • Global Codes across activities (e.g., positive behavior, negative behavior)
  • Activity-specific codes (e.g., ability to clarify a problem/hope, extent of reaching a solution)
